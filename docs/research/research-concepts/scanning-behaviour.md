Concept 03 — Scanning Behaviour
Evidence Register
Source 1 — Nielsen Norman Group (Eye-Tracking Research)

Eye-tracking studies consistently show that people generally scan web pages before deciding whether to read them. The classic F-pattern is one common behaviour on text-heavy pages, but later research found several scanning patterns depending on the user's goal, motivation, and page layout.

Source 2 — GOV.UK Content Design

The UK Government's content design guidance incorporates this research directly into its writing principles. It recommends writing for scanning because most users initially look for relevant information rather than reading every word. Clear headings, concise sections, and front-loaded information improve discoverability.

Source 3 — CDC Clear Writing Guidance

The CDC similarly advises that readers first scan a page and then decide whether it is worth reading in detail. Their guidance recommends structuring information so that the most important content is immediately visible and easy to locate.

Evidence-Based Concept

People do not usually consume digital information linearly.

Instead, they rapidly scan pages to determine:

Is this the information I need?
Where is the part relevant to me?
Is this worth reading in detail?

Reading typically occurs after scanning confirms that the page is relevant.
