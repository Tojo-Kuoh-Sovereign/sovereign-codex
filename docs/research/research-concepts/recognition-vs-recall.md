Concept 02 — Recognition vs. Recall
Evidence Register
Source 1 — Jakob Nielsen's Usability Heuristics

Nielsen's sixth usability heuristic is "Recognition rather than recall." The principle states that users should not have to remember information from one part of a system to another. Instead, interfaces should make objects, actions, and options visible so users can recognize them when needed.

Source 2 — Cognitive Psychology / Usability

Recognition and recall are different forms of memory retrieval.

Recognition = identifying something when it is presented.
Recall = retrieving information without external cues.

Recognition consistently requires less mental effort and is more reliable than recall. This is why menus, autocomplete, and visible options are generally easier to use than requiring users to remember commands or terminology.

Source 3 — Human-Computer Interaction

Recognition reduces the burden placed on working memory by keeping important information visible and available in context. Instead of expecting users to remember information between screens or pages, systems should provide cues at the point they are needed.

Evidence-Based Concept

People are generally better at recognising information than recalling it from memory.

Knowledge systems should therefore minimise situations where users must remember terminology, locations, or relationships without assistance.
